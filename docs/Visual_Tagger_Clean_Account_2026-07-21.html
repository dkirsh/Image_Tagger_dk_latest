The Visual Tagger — what it does, how it integrates, and where it stands

The image engine of the Cognitive + Wellness Code, its place in the Article Eater / Knowledge Atlas system, and the annotated-image library

Clean account · David Kirsh · 21 July 2026

1What the Visual Tagger does

The Visual Tagger reads a picture of a space — a photograph or, better, a render straight from the 3-D model with its materials — and returns a structured, evidence-grounded account of where that space helps or hurts the people in it: how easy it is to look at, how it will feel to move through, where a conversation stays private, where the light helps or glares, how safe each spot feels, how legible the path is. It turns a brief like "make it feel spectacular and welcoming" into something checkable while the design is still on the screen.

Two things make it more than a filter bank. First, it spatialises function: instead of one number per room (one reverberation time, an "open-plan" label) it returns fields — a map over the whole floor of where speech carries, where the glass puts a person on display, where a group can gather unheard, how far a threat can be seen coming. It answers where, not merely how much. Second, every reading is tiered for honesty and self-auditing: each attribute declares how it was computed, carries a confidence, and — for the audited ones — ships a cryptographic "sufficient-statistic" signature so a checker can prove the number was produced by the declared method on the declared image and was not fabricated, stale, or run by the wrong pipeline. A space that can't be read honestly abstains rather than guessing.

2How it integrates with Article Eater and the Knowledge Atlas

The Tagger is the measurement half of a larger system whose other half is evidence. They are being brought together so that every attribute reading arrives already grounded in the research literature, with its warrant and its uncertainty attached.

The through-line. Article Finder decides which papers are worth reading next; Article Eater reads them and structures each one, study by study, into a post-Quinean web of belief — nodes of claim joined by warrant links, organised into live debate clusters. That web is the Knowledge Atlas. The Visual Tagger then binds each of its 68 attributes to the Atlas nodes that justify it, so a reading is never a bare number: it is a number plus the vetted sources behind it, the strength of that science, and — where the Atlas can — a proposed neural mechanism. When the Atlas's value-of-information engine flags a gap the literature can't close, it proposes an experiment; that experiment is built in the Experiment Maker, run on stimuli drawn from the image corpus, and its human results feed back to calibrate the Tagger — closing the loop.

Concretely, integration means the Tagger becomes a module inside the Article Eater ecosystem rather than a standalone tool, joined at four seams:

1
Attribute → evidence binding. Each registry attribute (e.g. prospect, feature_congestion, circadian_contrast) carries a stable link to the Atlas belief-nodes and citations that license it. The tier the Tagger assigns and the confidence the Atlas assigns are shown together.
2
Grounded explanations. The Tagger's question-driven composer (the part that turns a reading into a plain-language answer) pulls its numbers only from the measurement side and its justifications only from the Atlas — kept strictly separate so an explanation can never invent a statistic.
3
Gap → experiment → calibration. Atlas research-gaps and the Tagger's un-validated (AMBER) attributes define the experiment queue; Experiment Maker + the corpus produce human labels; those labels fit the Tagger's thresholds and response constants and promote attributes from mechanically-green to validated-green.
4
Shared corpus & index. The annotated image library (§5) is the common substrate: stimuli for the experiments, exemplars for the Atlas's claims, and the searchable front door for everyone else.

Atlas scale today (David's figures): ~760 papers structured into ~522 fields, ~1,900 belief nodes joined by ~1,900 warrant links, ~38 live debate clusters, ~18 flagged research gaps, with a value-of-information engine choosing what to read next. The Tagger's registry has grown to 68 computed attributes.

The honest summary of "how far along." The machinery is largely built: 68 attributes compute, 28 self-audit, the pipeline is deterministic and reproducible, and the honesty discipline (abstain rather than guess; tier rather than overclaim) is in place. The proof is the work ahead: not one attribute has yet been calibrated against human judgement, so every "GREEN" is a green procedure, not a green measurement. Converting the 49 AMBER "honest procedures" into validated measurements is the dominant remaining task, and it is the one thing that needs people at scale — the corpus campaign and the POE pilot.

4The correctness ladder

An attribute climbs four rungs; keeping them distinct is what keeps the system honest:

1
Computational — a real, effective procedure exists (not a hand-wave). All 68.
2
Audited (M1′) — it emits a sufficient-statistic signature so a checker can prove the number is genuine, current, and produced by the declared method. 28 of 68; the rest queued.
3
Faithful — where it ports a published algorithm, it is adjudicated against the reference to ~1e-6. Done for the clutter core; the 2-D discomfort metric is next.
4
Validated — its output correlates with human (and biosignal) judgement of the construct it claims, with the threshold fit to data and the measured validity number recorded. Not yet reached by any attribute — this is the corpus + POE campaign.

5The annotated-image library — search & visual display

Around the Tagger sits a growing library of real interior images, each one annotated with its computed attribute profile, its tier, and its evidence links, and browsable both as pictures and as data. The Tagger's layered viewer can overlay any reading on the image itself — the light field, the speech-privacy field, the prospect/refuge map, the honesty layer — so a person sees not just that a space scores a certain way but where and why. The goal is a searchable, explainable front door to the whole system: ask a question in ordinary language or by filter, get back real spaces that answer it, each with its annotated overlays and its grounding.

How different people would use it:

A student designing an experiment

"Show me real interiors that are high vs low on prospect, matched on brightness and clutter." The library returns a ready stimulus set with known, computed attribute values and licence-clean images — so the student starts from validated, controlled stimuli instead of hunting Google Images, and can hand the set straight to the Experiment Maker.

An architect chasing a psych/neuro objective

"Find precedents that read as restorative and legible with strong daylight but low glare." They get exemplars that meet the objective, each with the attribute maps that explain it — or they drop in their own render and get its profile plus the cheapest fix named. Design ideas, evidenced.

An ordinary person

"Which of these two rooms is calmer, and why?" The composer answers in plain language, points at the specific zones driving it (the glare pool, the exposed seating), and never quotes a number it can't back — a genuinely explainable second opinion on a space.

An ANFA member (neuroscience & architecture)

"For prospect-refuge, show the construct, its measured validity, the citations and the debate, the spaces that best exemplify it, and the neural mechanism the Atlas proposes." Here the three layers converge: the Atlas grounding, the Tagger's measurement, and the corpus exemplars — a research-grade, source-traceable view that a scientist can interrogate and a designer can act on.

6Where we are, in one paragraph, and what's next

The Visual Tagger is a working, self-auditing instrument with 68 computed attributes across seven families, roughly half of them already carrying the honesty signature, a faithfully-ported clutter core, a reproducible pipeline, and a first annotated image corpus with a searchable index and a layered viewer. What it is not yet is validated: no attribute has been calibrated against human experience, and several (prospect-refuge, biophilia, the wellness variables that need spectral or measured input) need re-grounding or a hand-off to instruments. The near-term programme is therefore clear — finish the audit coverage and the faithful ports (engineering), run the human-labelling campaign on the corpus and the POE pilot on real rooms (validation), and complete the binding to the Knowledge Atlas so every reading arrives with its evidence. That is what turns a set of honest procedures into a code you can stand behind.
